A more convenient login

Insurance is a begrudging purchase that is typically set and forget, until you need it most.

In 2019 we looked to improve the security of our online self service platform by providing a more secure login system that didn't punish infrequent users of the platform, while also eliminating a barrier to entry by removing the password requirement altogether.

The Challenge

Our goal for this project was to increase the number of successful logins through the online policy management platform by reducing barriers to entry. In turn, this would contribute to solving a business need by reducing the number of users calling to make changes to their policy.

Scope and constraints

When we were handed this project some level of solutionising had already been completed, namely with the security and infrastructure teams to determine feasibility. The security team determined that SMS was the most secure method of sending a OTP (one-time pin), and as such that was the only method we would be proceeding with. This would become one of the most controversial decisions of the project.

My role

For this project I worked as a UX researcher during the research and discovery phase, as well as the UI designer for the prototypes and eventual implementation.

The Approach

At the outset of the project we didn't have any existing insights into the current journey, so we were unable to form clear goals. Our first task was to understand how users perceive the current journey, fortunately we have access to an abundance of data at our fingertips.

Early insights

I analyzed themes of data across many of our user feedback platforms to form an early picture of our users opinion of the current journey. From surveys, live chats, call recording, and contact forms we formed a picture of a login that according to our users either “just works” or “never works”. Some common themes included:

  • Clunky password reset process
  • Difficult to navigate and non responsive UI built without mobile in mind (which now makes up over 50% of our sales)

We needed to dive deeper to understand why the login was smooth for some users, and incredibly frustrating to other users.

“How might we make the login process easier so that existing users can manage their policy online themselves?”

Defining success

Given our challenge is to increase the number of successful logins, our definition of success for this project directly relates to this metric. OTPs are a relatively new way to login, so we understand there is an education piece within this project that would only be solved with time as users grow more familiar with the system. Therefore, our measures of success were:

  1. Increasing the number of successful logins to the online policy management platform
  2. Without greatly increasing the number of users chatting or calling for assistance
  3. Determining the best time to disable the legacy email and password login
  4. Increasing the overall NPS of the login system

How we tested

A user research project typically calls for multiple rounds of testing to iterate over designs and validate changes. We knew early on that this project would require more complex iterative testing that would depend on our ability to capture and review data from our production environment before making changes.

What we wanted to know:

  1. Can users use the new login with SMS authentication?
  2. What would improve the new login experience?
  3. What barriers to entry could be identified? (no signal, phone dead, old phone number)

User feedback analysis

To form a more in-depth picture of the current state I returned to analyzing feedback from existing users. This time I dove deeper than just general themes and started to log specific reasons for users failing to login.

We quickly found that:

Data analysis Insight #1

The login is not optimized for mobile design (over 50% of our traffic)

Data analysis Insight #2

The UI incorrectly implies that the email and password are correct, not just valid

Data analysis Insight #3

The reset password email is not consistently received

Data analysis Insight #4

Users frequently got locked out of their account, but insist that their password was correct (because they always use the same one, or they wrote it down, which are major security risks)

Data analysis Insight #5

Users are unsure whether they have previously used social media logins, and if they need to again if they have previously

These findings alongside our defined scope and measures for success allowed us to move from the early insights and discovery phase into the prototyping and testing phase of the project.

Testing and validation

One of our first questions was whether the call to action to login on budgetdirect.com.au was working as we intended. We ran an A/B test to determine if the “Policy Manager login” button text was the most effective call to action. In the first round we tested a number of different alternatives:

  • Control: “Policy manager login”
  • Variant 1: “Manage your account”
  • Variant 2: “Login”
  • Variant 3: “Existing user login”

We ran this test for 2 weeks and quickly determined variant 2 was the most successful option, with a 3% increase in users successfully logging into our online self service portal, as well as a 2% increase in transactions through the online portal.

Iterate

After seeing the success of the “login” variant we iterated. Now knowing that users prefer clear and concise labels, we tested “login” against a “sign in” call to action.

Over a 30 day test we confirmed our earlier findings, that “login” was the preferred label for users. We saw a 3.6% higher successful login rate than those users that saw the “sign in” option.

Exploratory user testing

With a prototype in hand we ran a group of exploratory moderated user testing sessions. In this test we asked users to imagine they were a user of Budget Direct and wanted to manage their policy online.

Recruitment

We used Brisbane based recruitment platform Askable to screen and recruit 6 participants representing an array of demographics that represent our typical user base. We split the tests across both mobile and desktop to get a true representation of how our users typically interact with out platforms.

Set up

I translated the prototype from AdobeXD into Axure to give the participants a truly interactive experience with our prototype. Axure allows us to capture and process information entered, so we were able to provide a real representation of what the finished product would look and feel like.

For the purposes of the test we mocked up a mobile phone with an example of an authentication SMS that a user might receive. We provided the mock up as a paper print out and asked the user to image they had received the SMS on their personal mobile phone.

Test

We followed a typical moderated user testing format

  1. Introduction and rapport building
  2. Set the scene - “you need to make a car insurance claim right now, what would you do?”
  3. Observe
  4. Once the user found the login button we switched to a prototype and let them continue
  5. After their successful login we wrapped this section up with some questions around their general sentiment towards to process
    • What did you think of the process?
    • Would you use it? If not what would make you want to use it?
    • How secure do you think it is? Why?
    • Do you feel secure using your phone number for identification?

The outcome

  • No one had a problem!
  • Everyone got through with no errors
  • They recognised the PIN process from other websites eg. Banking
  • Generally, they didn't have any security concerns
  • People generally didn't notice the difference between the fist login and the second
  • Because people enter from different places there is an opportunity to make the process smoother by personalising the title (eg: claiming, maintain their policy)

Gather and prioritise

With a growing collection of information from user feedback analysis as well as user testing we began to group themes amongst our findings and prioritized them based on the impact to the user journey. We emphasized the problems that would completely prevent users from logging in, as they went against our earlier defined goals.

Present to the business

From there we presented our findings back to the business, providing key stakeholders with much needed insights into the new journey and our findings. We recommended changes and important pieces of work that should be completed prior to go-live. Armed with this information, the business decided to move forward with their current solution, regardless of our findings.

The business also wanted to decommission the legacy email and password login in tandem with the release of the OTP login. We strongly advised against this as we had no idea how successful the new OTP login would be, and how dependent users would be on this legacy login. We suggested monitoring the success rate and impact on chat periodically to determine when our users would be ready for such a change, to which the business agreed.

The Findings

user Insight #1

No fallbacks

  • When the user fails to receive the SMS
  • When the contact center is unavailable to fix the account
  • When the user is overseas (either permanently or on holiday)
  • When the users mobile number has changed
  • When the user doesn't have a mobile phone
  • When their mobile phone is flat or broken
  • When the user lives in a part of Australia with poor signal
user Insight #2

No consideration for user security preferences

  • Many users find passwords more secure (especially those with password managers)
  • user expect to be able to use email to receive their code
user Insight #3

No recovery methods for users whose details don't exactly match what we have saved

  • Shortened first names (Mat instead of Matthew), exacerbated by our conversational quote questions
  • Typos by consultants
  • Missing punctuation such as apostrophes
user Insight #4

No consideration for users in unconventional arrangements

  • Children managing their elderly parents' policies
  • Personal Assistants looking after their managers company policy

Decommissioning the legacy login

We began to monitor the success of the OTP login, and through A/B test we periodically assessed the impact of removing the legacy login.

  • Test 2 (08/2020): -23.9% successful logins, +28% increase in chats
  • Test 3 (04/2021): -53% successful logins, +18.7% increase in chats
  • Test 4 (02/2022): +0.9% successful logins, -6% decrease in chats

Finally, after 2 years of testing we felt comfortable removing the legacy email and password login, seeing from these results that we would not be adversely affecting the login rate, or increase the amount of contact into the contact centre.

The next steps

There is always more work to be done, and now we need to work with the security team to provide an alternative method for receiving the code in order to close the last gap we identified.
