Care to compare

Understanding and comparing benefits of insurance policies can often cause consumers more confusion than clarity.

In 2021 we looked to improve the major decision point of our online quote and sale journey to assist users in comparing cover to ensure they select the right insurer for them.

The Challenge

In the 20 years since its Australian launch, Budget Direct Insurance has grown to become one of the biggest challengers in the Australian Insurance market.

As insurance becomes more complicated users look for more ways to make sense of all the inclusions and exclusions, and the cost verses the benefits of each policy. That creates a need to ensure their main decision point, the price page, provides them with their ideal user experience.

In this project our goal was to improve the conversion rate of this main decision point, in addition to reducing the need for users to contact us for clarification.

Scope and constraints

Due to development team scheduling our scope was restricted to meaningful changes we could make on this specific page.

My role

For this project I worked as a UX researcher during the research and discovery phase, as well as part of the UI team on the design.

The Approach

At the outset of the project we knew a lot about how users historically interacted with this page, but not a lot about what todays users actual want from it.

Early insights

Armed with an arsenal of data, we poured through user chats, phone calls, feedback surveys and complaints forms to understand the current landscape and where we were weakest. What we found:

Data Insight #1

Users skim. A lot.

We assumed that users read what we presented to them. We were wrong. Purchasing insurance is a necessity, not a delight. As such, we found that users skimmed the journey just enough to answer the questions, but became frustrated if they couldn't find answers to their own questions easily.

Data Insight #2

Users aren't sure what they want covered.

Most users haven't had to claim for Home Insurance, so they don't know what they actually want to be covered for. They just want the best value for money.

Data Insight #3

Users want to easily compare policies across companies.

They want us to tell them where we fall short and where we exceed other companies so they can decide what is valuable to themselves, and what they can live without.

Data Insight #4

Users don't want us to waste their time.

users don't want their valuable time wasted on things they consider pointless, such as awards, marketing etc. they want us to get straight to the point so they can get it over and done with.

“How might we improve the likelihood of users to continue past the price page without changing product pricing?”

Defining success

At the end of the day success for the business is an increase is genuine sales, without an increase in cancellations. This goes hand in hand with the user goal of purchasing a policy that is right for them, without the price tag.

For the UX team our goal was to improve user understanding on this key decision point in the journey. By doing so, we should see an increase in genuine users and a decrease is confused users purchasing a policy that wasn't right for them, and then needing to cancel via the contact centre.

Reframing the problem

Competitor analysis informed us that we were already very competitive in the price space, and benefits were typically the same across different brands. We could only assume that brand perception and the information and language throughout our journey contributed to a perception that our product or brand is inferior to competitors.

With this knowledge we decided to reframe the problem to highlight that our need was now:


“How might we better inform users of the benefits of our policies without overloading the quote process?”

How we tested

Three major questions informed my design strategy for this project:

  1. How do you design for everyone?
  2. What is the most valuable information according to the users?
  3. What would make them continue?

What do you want from me?

It was important for us to understand the why behind what the users were saying in our early insights. We knew what they thought they wanted, but why did they want it? Could we solve their concerns in a better way?

What is all this?

The existing price page was a jumble of insurance lingo, legally required disclosures, marketing pitches and a list of what you've already answered. Where was the value for our users?

In order to understand what users were actually looking for we needed to speak to actual users.

How does the need differ across our diverse users?

We used Brisbane based recruitment agency Askable to find participants for in person interviews for our Home Quote journey. We selected participants that covered a wide range of our home owner user base. From young to old, first time and seasoned insurance purchasers, across all genders.

We know from data that 54% of our users use mobile devices, so we tested half our participants on mobile while the other half used desktop to ensure we weren't missing device specific problems.

The process

We asked participants to complete a three part moderated user interview, which followed this structure:

  1. Introductions and background on the participants demographic
  2. Get a quote (to provide context to the price page and tease out the type of user they are)
  3. Think and comment on the current price pages successes and misses
  4. Compare and contrast to the new price page design
  5. Paper prototype build their ideal price page
  6. Card sort to prioritize benefits

STEP 1: Introductions

We introduce the UX researchers and ask the participant to tell us a bit about themselves, giving them time to become more comfortable with the facilitators. This also gives us valuable insights into the type of purchaser they might be, and what they value from an insurance policy.

STEP 2: Get a quote

The participant demonstrates how they would typically find, compare and purchase an insurance policy. This might be through Google, a comparison website, or direct to a brand they know. We observed their research process and what information was truly valuable to them.

STEP 3: Current state price page

We ask the participant to pause their journey at the price page and take a deeper look. We have some probing questions ready for any participants that have trouble thinking out loud, for example:

  • What do you like about this page? What do you dislike?
  • What is missing from this page?
  • Is there anything you would remove all together?

STEP 4: Compare and contrast

We show the participant an interactive prototype of the new design of this page and ask them to repeat the previous step.

STEP 5: Paper prototyping

After seeing both versions of the page and hearing their thoughts, we ask the participant to craft their ideal price page with the pieces of the original designs, as well as sticky notes for anything new they'd like to add.

STEP 6: Prioritizing benefits

We asked the participants to perform some card sorting in order to prioritise which benefits mean more to them. We also asked that they leave out any benefits they simply do not care about at all.

The insights

While we found many areas for improvement, there were some consistent themes throughout the test that we really needed to address.

User Interviews Insight #1

Show me that you care.

users want us to care about them. They want to see through the UI that we care about their circumstances, and that we had taken every unique part of them into consideration. They want to know they aren't just a number, and they want us to take the time to explain the minutia of the policy to them, if they so choose.

That begs the question, how do you serve a user without knowing what kind of user they are? How can you hold the hand of a first time purchaser, but reduce friction of an experienced purchaser?

User Interviews Insight #2

You know I don't understand.

From existing data we know that users don't understand or misinterpret the “legal stuff” of an insurance policy, and those are typically key to a claim being approved. users want to understand their policy, but we make it too hard for them by hiding behind legal terms and confusing names.

We know that users don't understand these terms, but the industry standard is to use them anyway to cover ourselves in case of legal issues. So why don't we listen to users when they tell us they don't understand? Why can't we use more human friendly text to truly explain the policy to our users, instead of accepting that it's too hard, or not our problem?

User Interviews Insight #3

How much is too much?

The full list of benefits (that we incorrectly assumed would be a positive) actually overwhelmed participants. They're telling us they want to know everything they're covered for, but when we give it to them, it's too much. How might we group or consolidate the benefits to convey how much is covered without overwhelming the users?

User Interviews Insight #4

Isn't this the end?

Every. Single. Participant. Thought the price page was the end, and after that they would be paying. This is a huge misconception, as more than half the journey is still to come, with almost all pages having an impact on price. How can we convey to users that this is an indicative price, but they've still got more to go?

The impact

Due to time constraints within the Home Product team we pivoted to test the Car Product first. Our internal A/B test team ran a 50/50 test using the updated new design against the current version for the Car Product and we saw some significant wins, such as:

  • Reduction in % Offline Sales resulting from online quotes (-16.4%)
  • Increase in % Agreed Value sales (+14.5%)
  • Increase in add-ons (% Hire Car Option +5.5%, % Choice of Repairer +9.4, % Windscreen Cover +3.2%)

It wasn't all good news

We did see an increase in the number of users selecting an annual payment frequency (+3.8%), and a larger percentage of those users leaving before purchasing. We attributed this change to the new design defaulting to annual payment frequencies instead of requiring a selection.

Run it back

To confirm this theory, we ran another A/B test. This time we tested the new design against an updated version that required the user to select a payment frequency. The results showed the updated design increased conversion but had no impact on the prior positive results.


We have a winner.
