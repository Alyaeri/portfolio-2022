The ultimate design system

As any business scales it finds itself adding more. More functionality, more features, more staff. As our web platforms took off the business began expanding into other insurance products, which included expanding the teams in order to scale rapidly. With this expansion we lost the strict oversight we once had, relying on the teams to follow the correct practices.

The Challenge

As we reflect a number of years later, we can see that the rapid expansion left us with the illusion of consistency, when in reality it was all but. We had products that had copied the library (not branched) so far in the past that they were no longer compatible for updates, as well as many with styling overrides and fixes that only applied to their own version.

The Design System project aims to unify all the products into one manageable ecosystem, paving the way for updates across the whole system that enables us to meet our goal of AA web accessibility standards.

Scope and constraints

8 products, many with over 100 pages, and endless bespoke UIs that needed to be inventoried and consolidated. On top of that, our products are white labeled and sold as other brands, so we needed to enable enough configuration and customization to match the look and feel of 12 partner brands.

My role

I spearheaded this project as the lead UI designer, as well as the UX and web accessibility champion. For this project I worked closely with both the Research & Development team, as well as the dev teams.

The Approach

First came the painstaking process of sifting through hundreds and hundreds of pages of UI across 8 very different insurance apps to identify and screenshot every element we used. Every variance was captured, from an extra icon here, to a different font size there. I saved it all.

Group.

Armed with hundreds of unique UI elements I began to group them into categories. Seeing them all side by side truly highlighted our need for a design system, how could we ever manage all of these apps when they were all so varied? We had to unify, and these were our candidate components.

Consolidate.

Using these categories I began to build a landscape of what components we would actually need across all our apps. Identifying bespoke UIs that could be catered to with another component we had already used elsewhere. I began to trim the fat.

Consider.

At this point I began to consider not only what we could remove, but what we might add as well. This was our opportunity to redefine our UI and build more flexibility into what was currently a very inflexible design system.

I am a big advocate for accessible design, and this felt like the perfect opportunity to push for inclusive design within our ecosystem. If we were rebuilding the UI from scratch, why not make it accessible while we're at it? After all, accessible design isn't just for the disabled or impaired, it sets the standard for a UI with hierarchy and purpose, that is accessible for everyone. And when you're in an industry that serves the general population, you need to understand and design for the complexity that comes with every unique human.

“How might we reduce the complexity in maintaining our UI by leveraging reusable designs?”

Defining success

Success was measuring differently across the teams invested in the design system.

For the design team

For the design team success meant having a design system we could lean on to facilitate rapid prototyping. It also gave us a tool to push back on business requests for bespoke UIs by asking them to quantify the value in the extra development time.

For the development team

For the development teams, success meant reducing the time they spent on front end work by leveraging reusable components to rapidly build web screens. It also allowed the QA testers to verify UIs without involving a designer.

For the business

For the business success meant having less tech stacks to manage and maintain, allowing for faster development time and a more consistent look and feel. It also meant adding the functionality to allow us to truly white label our products, and use our newly found AA standard as a selling point in conversations with proposed partners.

Broadening the scope

A design system is more than just a component library, it's an ecosystem. A set of principles, processes and systems to manage design at scale. As such, we needed to think beyond just the UI, widening our scope to set the standard for our design system as a whole. Our goals became:

  1. Reusable components
  2. Meets AA web accessibility standards
  3. Cohesive with our existing brochureware website
  4. Expands customization for white labelled partners
  5. Facilitates more comprehensive UI testing by our manual testers
  6. Allows for automated UI testing

The solution

With our widened scope in mind I began researching Component Library structures, how might we hang together our huge number of components in a flexible pattern that reduces complexity but enables us to maintain some very intricate components? We needed a universal language that both the designers and developers could speak.

I quickly found Atomic Design by Brad Frost and it truly resonated with me. In his blog he explains that “Atomic design is a methodology for creating design systems. There are five distinct levels in atomic design:”

  1. Atoms, the basic building blocks of matter
  2. Molecules, groups of atoms bonded together and are the smallest fundamental units of a compound.
  3. Organisms, groups of molecules joined together to form a relatively complex, distinct section of an interface.
  4. Templates, Templates consist mostly of groups of organisms stitched together to form pages.
  5. Pages, are specific instances of templates

The concept being that every component in our library could be broken down until they became atoms. Conversely, every intricate component could be built on a foundation of atoms and molecules. Atomic Design has become our foundation for the design system, with every component assigned a relative level based on their practical implementation.

Creating a process

It wasn't enough to create a new library of components, we needed a defined process for the creation of new components to prevent us ending up right back where we started.

  1. Identify. When the need for a new component is identified
  2. Evaluate. What does this component need to do? How does it differ to our existing components? Could it be handled by extending an existing component?
  3. Assess. If we've determined we need to create a new component to fill this need then I will assess the required functionality and look for any reusable patterns we could leverage.
  4. Create. Design and define the new component states and functionality
  5. Review. Provide the Component library development team with a chance to review the design and provide feedback on feasibility or concerns. Also gives stakeholders a chance to review the new component and identify gaps.
  6. Iterate. Repeat steps 4 – 6 until design, development and stakeholders are satisfied with the proposed component.
  7. Publish. Publish new component definition to our hosted Component Library prototype via AdobeXD.
  8. Handover. Export detailed specifications from AdobeXD to Zeplin and provide to development team.
  9. Build. Component is built by developers and published to Storybook for testing and UI verification.
  10. Verify. Through Storybook the test team verifies the component is working as expected, and BackstopJS ensures no visual regression for existing components.
  11. Sign off. Completed component is signed off by testers and UI team, and then becomes available for use by the wider development teams.

Building a library

With a design system locked in and a process in place, I pushed forward with painstakingly creating 60+ components (and growing all the time!) in AdobeXD. Many components require designs for each of their potential states (for example, :active, :hover, :disabled, :focus), which resulted in hundreds of designs.

It was a lot. A lot.

Making it white labelable

Given our wider scope also required us to “Expands customization for white labelled partners“, I also spent time mapping the scss variables we would need and their values for each partner brand. Through the component library we significantly increased the amount of customization each partner could apply to our UI to make it look and feel more like their own, without increasing the maintenance overhead.

A more inclusive design

My major goal for this project was to meet AA Web Accessibility Standards. As a modern business that caters to the masses it's truly disgraceful that we weren't already meeting these basic web standards. I advocated hard to include the requirement to meet AA standards in our design system project. I was able to convince my manager and associate director of the value we would create, and got the green light to build towards AA standards.

From a business perspective meeting AA standards will always be a positive when working with our partner brands, as it can only aid in making our UI easier to use.

It was a no brainer.

Smoother developer handover

In the past each team has handled developer handover differently, making the designers job more complex than it needed to be. By migrating to Zeplin we have streamlined the developer handover by providing detailed specifications at the touch of a button. With Zeplins 'Connected Components' we were able to link components it a prototype directly to the real component in Storybook, taking away the guesswork and explicitly telling developers exactly which component to use.

Facilitating UI testing

Another perk of the design system was its ability to facilitate UI testing through manual and automated testing.

Automated testing

BackstopJS has been a godsend for the Component library. It completes automated visual regression testing for UIs across configured devices and operating systems to prevent unintended changes being pushed to the Component Library. This is significantly reduced the amount of UI sign off the design team needs to complete, and removes us as a bottleneck to development of components.

Streamlining manual testing

Zeplin arms the manual test team with UI testing tools to compare the design to the built component and identify gaps or mistakes before the component makes it to production. Screen overlay, detailed specifications and versioning of designs means that manual testers can verify UIs on their own, without waiting for UI designer sign off.

The impact

The work is never done, and the Design System will always be evolving. However, with strong processes and controls in place, alongside better working processes that reduce the overhead for the teams, this Design System is here to stay.

For the design team

We have a design system we can rely on. We have detailed component definitions built in AdobeXD that directly connect to real components in Storybook, allowing us to prototype at speeds we were never capable of before. We have a resource that facilitates discussions with the business, and a process that allows us to gatekeep the design system to ensure that we spend our time building components that genuinely deliver value.

For the development teams

The Design System project has been a phenomenal success. Teams are seeing a huge return on investment by migrating to the Component Library, and new apps are all slated to be built using the new design system. Existing products are scheduling sprints to complete their upgrade as soon as possible, as the benefits speak for themselves.

For the business

The business is starting to see a real return on investment as products migrate to the new library and UI related bugs being to taper off. More technical work can be completed as teams no longer need to worry about maintaining the UI, giving them more time to focus on the complex stuff.
